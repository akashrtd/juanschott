## 1. Purpose

This document defines what is authoritative in the JuanSchott lore and what can be changed. It establishes a tiered hierarchy for all narrative and worldbuilding content, governance rules for modifying each tier, and conflict resolution procedures when sources contradict.

The canon hierarchy exists to solve a specific problem in a living, seasonal game: the lore grows and changes over time. Without a clear authority structure, contradictions accumulate, writers conflict with each other, and the world loses coherence. This document prevents that.

Every piece of JuanSchott lore — from the core premise to a joke in a patch note — exists somewhere in this hierarchy. Knowing where it sits tells you how permanent it is and what it takes to change it.

---

## 2. Canon Tiers

The hierarchy consists of five tiers, ordered from most authoritative to least. Higher tiers override lower tiers in any conflict.

### Tier 1: Core Canon

**Cannot be changed without breaking the game's identity.**

Core Canon is the bedrock. These are the foundational truths that define what JuanSchott is. Removing or altering any Core Canon element would require rebuilding the game from a different premise. Core Canon is set at the inception of the project and is intended to be permanent.

The following elements are Core Canon:

- **The Vimana exists.** A cylindrical megastructure satellite operated by the Pantheon. It appears in Earth's orbit cyclically. It is real, physical, and undeniable.
- **The Pantheon exists.** A collective of powerful, godlike beings who operate the Vimana. They are not metaphorical. They are real entities within the fiction.
- **The Cycle system.** The Vimana arrives, the Invitation is sent, the competition occurs, judgment is delivered, the Vimana departs. This cycle is the structural backbone of both the lore and the live-service model.
- **Earth is damaged.** The Earth that humanity inhabits is not a thriving utopia. It has been harmed — the specifics of how and how much are lower-tier canon, but the fact of damage is Core Canon. This is why the Vimana matters.
- **The four species exist.** Humans, Cybersymbiotes, and two others. These are the playable species. They are real, distinct, and coexist within the fiction.
- **JuanSchott is the protagonist.** The figure who first entered the Vimana and returned. The game bears this name. The character exists within the fiction.
- **Low TTK.** The game is designed around low time-to-kill. This is a gameplay identity element that is also reflected in the fiction — combat in the Vimana is fast and lethal. Pantheon technology makes this possible.
- **The core mechanics.** Movement abilities (sprint, slide, wall-run, wall jump, double jump), weapon types (hitscan, projectile, beam), health regeneration, the add-on system. These are the mechanical identity of the game.
- **The competitive framework.** Players enter the Vimana to compete. The competition is the game. There are no PvE elements in the core loop. The fiction supports a competitive arena.
- **Cosmetic-only monetization and progression.** No pay-to-win. No gameplay-affecting purchases. This is a foundational design and business principle.

**Governance**: Core Canon changes require **unanimous agreement from all contributors** (design lead, narrative lead, technical lead, and project owner). A Core Canon change is a project-defining event. It is documented in the decisions log with full rationale. Core Canon changes are expected to be extremely rare — ideally never.

**Examples of Core Canon changes (hypothetical, for illustration)**:
- Removing the Vimana from the fiction (would require a completely different game).
- Adding PvE as a core game mode (would change the fundamental identity).
- Changing to a class-based system with fixed loadouts (would alter the core mechanical identity).

### Tier 2: Established Canon

**Published lore that is authoritative but could be revised if necessary with good reason.**

Established Canon is lore that has been written, published (in documents, in-game content, or official communications), and is treated as true. It is not as immutable as Core Canon, but changing it requires justification and transparency. Established Canon provides the detail and texture that makes the world feel real and consistent.

The following elements are Established Canon:

- **The five Pantheon members.** The Architect, The Flame, The Void, The Weaver, The Anvil. Their names, domains, and general personalities are established. Specific dialogue or minor personality traits could shift, but the five members and their core identities are fixed.
- **The reward history.** What has happened in past Cycles. Specific events that are referenced in lore have already occurred and are part of the timeline.
- **Specific historical events.** Key moments in the timeline — the first arrival of the Vimana, the first human to enter, the discovery of the Cybersymbiote bonding process. These events anchor the timeline.
- **The nature of the Invitation.** A mental transmission sent to all humans when the Vimana arrives. The Invitation is impossible to ignore. This detail is established and affects how the fiction works.
- **The structure of the Vimana.** Cylindrical megastructure. Hub surface with artificial gravity. Interior sections including zero-g areas. This spatial arrangement is established.

**Governance**: Established Canon changes require **documented rationale and community discussion**. The change must be:
1. Proposed in writing with the specific element to be changed and the reason.
2. Discussed with all contributors (minimum: narrative lead, design lead).
3. Documented in the decisions log before implementation.
4. Communicated to the player community if the change affects published content.

Established Canon changes are expected to be rare but possible. They occur when the creative needs of a season or expansion conflict with a previously established detail, and the creative benefit of the change outweighs the cost of inconsistency.

**Examples of potential Established Canon changes**:
- Expanding the Pantheon from five to six members (would require careful integration with existing lore).
- Revealing that a "historical event" was misunderstood or misrecorded (a deliberate retcon with narrative purpose).
- Changing a Pantheon member's domain to better serve a season's theme (with in-universe justification).

### Tier 3: Soft Canon

**Lore that is suggestive, not definitive. Can be revised between seasons.**

Soft Canon is the flexible layer. It provides color, depth, and narrative richness but is not load-bearing. Soft Canon can be changed, contradicted, or overwritten without breaking the world's coherence. It is the sandbox where the narrative team operates freely.

Soft Canon includes:

- **Character backstories (archetypes).** The general background of the four species, their cultures, and their relationship to the Vimana. These are suggestions and starting points, not rigid history. A character's backstory can be revised if a better story emerges.
- **Specific Cycle outcomes (numbers TBD).** How many humans competed in a given Cycle, the specific outcomes of past competitions, the names of past competitors. These details are illustrative until locked into Established Canon by explicit reference.
- **Cultural details.** How Cybersymbiotes relate to their bonded machines. How human society is organized post-damage. How the four species interact socially. These details enrich the world but can evolve.
- **Pantheon member personalities (specifics).** While the five members and their core domains are Established Canon, the specific nuances of their personalities, their relationships with each other, and their exact dialogue are Soft Canon. A Pantheon member may speak differently in Season 3 than they did in Season 1, as the writers refine their voice.
- **Environmental storytelling details.** The specific appearance of a map element, the graffiti on a wall, the design of a terminal interface — these are Soft Canon. They convey a feeling, not a fact.

**Governance**: Soft Canon **can be revised by any season narrative**. No formal process is required. The narrative lead has authority to revise Soft Canon as needed for a season's story. Changes should be internally consistent within a season (do not contradict yourself within the same season) but may contradict Soft Canon from a previous season.

**No documentation is required for Soft Canon changes**, but the narrative lead should be aware of what is being changed and why, to avoid unnecessary churn.

### Tier 4: Flexible Canon

**Seasonal content. Each season introduces new lore that becomes Soft Canon after the season ends.**

Flexible Canon is the actively evolving layer. It is the lore of the current season — the Pantheon host's behavior, the map-specific story, the events of this particular Cycle. While a season is active, its Flexible Canon is treated as current truth. When the season ends, the Flexible Canon settles into Soft Canon (if it was well-received and coherent) or is quietly revised or forgotten (if it was not).

Flexible Canon includes:

- **Pantheon host behavior this season.** How the host acts, what they say, what judgments they make. This is specific to the current season and may or may not carry forward.
- **Map-specific stories.** The narrative reason for this season's map changes or additions. Why this section of the Vimana is now accessible or different.
- **Season-specific events.** Things that happen during this Cycle that are not guaranteed to be permanent additions to the lore.
- **New character or species details introduced by the season.** If a season reveals new information about a species or the Pantheon, that information is Flexible Canon until it is reinforced by a second source (at which point it may be promoted to Soft or Established Canon).

**Governance**: Flexible Canon is **managed by the narrative lead for each season**. The season's writer has full authority over Flexible Canon within the season. No cross-approval is needed.

**Lifecycle**:
1. Season begins. Flexible Canon is introduced.
2. Season runs. Flexible Canon is treated as current truth.
3. Season ends. Flexible Canon is evaluated:
   - **Retained**: Moved to Soft Canon. The lore element becomes part of the world's background.
   - **Revised**: Adjusted and then moved to Soft Canon. The lore element was a good idea but needs refinement.
   - **Dropped**: Not carried forward. The lore element was specific to this season and is not referenced again. This is not a retcon — it is simply a detail that is no longer relevant.

### Tier 5: Non-Canon

**Never referenced as authoritative.**

Non-Canon encompasses everything that is not governed by the canon hierarchy. It exists outside the fiction and should never be cited as a source of truth about the JuanSchott world.

Non-Canon includes:

- **Community content.** Fan fiction, fan art, community-created lore. The community's interpretation of the world is valued but is not authoritative.
- **Jokes and memes.** Humor derived from the game or its community. A funny community nickname for a Pantheon member is not their actual name.
- **April Fools events.** Any content released as an April Fools joke or explicitly labeled as non-serious. Even if it is published officially, it is Non-Canon unless explicitly promoted to a higher tier.
- **Developer commentary.** What developers say on Discord, in interviews, or in casual conversation about the lore. Developer intent is interesting but is not canon until it is published in a canonical document or in-game content.
- **Cut content.** Lore that was written but not published. Drafts, rejected ideas, abandoned storylines. If it was never published, it does not exist in the canon.

**Governance**: Non-Canon has **no governance**. It is not tracked, not referenced, and not constrained. The only rule for Non-Canon is that it must not be presented as canonical. Official communications should not cite Non-Canon as if it were authoritative.

## 3. Tier Promotion and Demotion

Lore elements can move between tiers, but only in specific directions and with specific processes.

### Promotion (Moving Up)

| From | To | Process |
|---|---|---|
| Non-Canon | Flexible Canon | Publish the content as part of a season. |
| Flexible Canon | Soft Canon | Retain the content after a season ends without revision. |
| Soft Canon | Established Canon | Publish the content in an authoritative document (world overview, codex) and reference it in at least two seasons without contradiction. |
| Established Canon | Core Canon | Unanimous contributor agreement. This has never happened and is not expected to. |

### Demotion (Moving Down)

| From | To | Process |
|---|---|---|
| Flexible Canon | Non-Canon | Drop the content at season end without carrying it forward. |
| Soft Canon | Flexible Canon | A season explicitly revises the detail, effectively reopening it for the season. |
| Established Canon | Soft Canon | Documented rationale, contributor discussion, decisions log entry, community communication. |
| Core Canon | (anything) | Cannot be demoted. Core Canon changes require redefining the element as no longer Core Canon, which is effectively the same as removing it. |

Demotion is more common than promotion. As the world grows, some details that seemed important become less so, and the hierarchy should reflect that flexibility.

## 4. Conflict Resolution

When two lore sources contradict each other, the following rules resolve the conflict, in order:

### Rule 1: Higher Tier Wins

If a Tier 2 source and a Tier 3 source contradict, the Tier 2 source is correct. Tier always takes precedence.

### Rule 2: More Recent Wins (Same Tier)

If two sources at the same tier contradict, the more recent source is correct. Lore evolves, and the latest published version of a detail is the current truth.

### Rule 3: Lore Bible Wins (Same Tier, Same Recency)

If two sources at the same tier with the same recency contradict (e.g., two documents published in the same week), defer to the **lore bible** (`world_overview.md`). The lore bible is the master narrative document and has implicit authority within its tier as the canonical summary of the world.

### Rule 4: Escalation

If the above rules do not resolve the conflict (e.g., the contradiction is between two Core Canon elements, which should not be possible), the conflict is escalated to the narrative lead and design lead for a ruling. The ruling is documented in the decisions log and becomes canonical.

## 6. Practical Guidelines

### For Writers

- **When writing seasonal content, start at Tier 4 (Flexible Canon).** Your season's story is yours to tell. Reference Established and Core Canon for consistency, but do not feel bound by Soft Canon.
- **If you want to change Established Canon, raise it before writing.** Do not publish content that contradicts Tier 2 without going through the governance process. You will be asked to revise.
- **Do not reference Non-Canon as if it were true.** If a community theory is cool and you want to make it real, promote it through the proper process (publish it as Flexible Canon in a season).

### For Designers

- **Gameplay mechanics are Core Canon.** If a lore element depends on a specific mechanic being true (e.g., "the low TTK is because Pantheon weapons disintegrate matter"), that lore element is reinforcing Core Canon and should be treated with corresponding care.
- **Seasonal modifiers are Flexible Canon.** The gameplay modifier is an expression of the Pantheon host's will. The modifier exists because the host made it so. When the season ends, the modifier ends. This is canon.

### For Developers

- **Code does not define canon.** If a technical limitation causes a gameplay behavior that contradicts lore, the lore is correct and the behavior is a bug (or at least a known limitation). Document the discrepancy.
- **Asset files do not define canon.** A texture or model file may contain details that are not canonical. The design documents, not the assets, are the source of truth.
