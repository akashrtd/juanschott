## Preamble

The Pantheon's reward system has done more than advance human technology — it has fractured the definition of humanity itself. Over centuries of genetic modification, bio-engineering, and cybernetic integration, what was once a single species has diverged into four recognizable branches. All are human in origin. All share the same fundamental biology at its root. But the accumulated effects of Pantheon rewards have pushed each branch in a distinct direction.

This document establishes the lore, culture, and visual identity of the four playable species in JuanSchott. From a gameplay perspective, all species share the same base frame for balance purposes. Species differences are expressed through narrative design, visual appearance, and backstory — never through mechanical advantage.

## 1. Humans (Baseline)

### Origin

Unmodified *Homo sapiens*. The original form. Every other playable species traces its lineage back to the human baseline. Humans are the control group against which all Pantheon-driven divergence is measured — and in many ways, they are the standard against which all other species measure themselves.

Humans have existed as long as humanity itself. They are not a product of Pantheon reward technology. They are the substrate from which all other species were derived. This gives them a unique position in the cultural hierarchy: they are simultaneously the most "ordinary" species and the one that all others are defined in relation to.

### Physiology

Standard human physiology with no inherent enhancements. Natural height, weight, and strength ranges. Sensory capabilities within baseline parameters. Reproduction through conventional biological means.

Natural lifespan has been extended significantly by Pantheon medical rewards — clean cancer cures, cellular repair therapies, organ regeneration. A baseline human in good standing with a Pantheon patron can reasonably expect 120 to 150 years of healthy life. But this is medical extension, not biological modification. The underlying machine is still standard human.

Some humans choose to refuse even medical rewards, maintaining their biology in its unaltered state. These individuals are a minority but a visible one. They are sometimes called "purists" or "anchorborn," and their choice carries cultural weight far beyond its practical significance.

### Culture

Humans are the majority population across every inhabited territory. They occupy every social stratum — from the lowest levels of unrewarded poverty to the highest echelons of Pantheon-favored privilege. There is no unified human culture. Humans are defined by their diversity of belief, tradition, and perspective.

What binds humans together is not a shared culture but a shared condition: the experience of being unmodified in a world that rewards modification. Some humans view bio-enhancement with deep suspicion, seeing it as a betrayal of the natural form. Others embrace it enthusiastically, pursuing every reward the Pantheon offers. Most fall somewhere in between — practical enough to accept medical rewards, cautious enough to draw lines elsewhere.

Human communities tend to be the most culturally varied. Their neighborhoods, guilds, and social structures reflect the full spectrum of human experience. This makes human-majority settlements the most politically complex — and often the most vibrant.

### Combat Role

Versatile and adaptable. Humans have no specializations and no inherent weaknesses. They are the universal baseline.

In the context of the Vimana cycle and arena combat, human fighters are valued for their unpredictability. A humanoid's enhanced reflexes can be accounted for. An artificial human's peak performance can be modeled. A cybersymbiote's processing speed can be anticipated. But a human fighter's limits are natural limits — and natural limits are variable. A human at full adrenaline-driven exertion can briefly match modified performance. A human fighting with genuine desperation is the hardest opponent to predict, because their performance envelope is bounded by will rather than design.

Human combat instructors are among the most respected in any guild, precisely because they must achieve results through technique, strategy, and training rather than biological advantage. A human who has survived multiple Vimana cycles is not merely experienced — they have proven that raw will can stand against engineered perfection.

### Why They Fight

The most diverse motivations of any species. Humans fight for duty, honor, community expectation, personal ambition, financial necessity, religious devotion, sheer love of combat, and every other reason that a thinking being can construct. No single motivation dominates. No generalization holds for long.

This motivational diversity is itself an advantage. It makes human fighters harder to psychologically profile. A humanoid fights to prove their worth. An artificial human fights because they were designed for it. A cybersymbiote fights because the symbiote demands it. But a human? A human's reasons are their own, and they change from one fighter to the next.

### Visual Identity

Natural proportions. Varied skin tones across the full human range. Recognizable facial features — asymmetries, imperfections, the thousand small variations that make each face unique. No glowing parts. No mechanical components. No unusual physical traits.

Humans are the most "readable" species. Their emotional states are visible in their faces and body language. Their physical condition is apparent at a glance. They do not conceal their exhaustion, their fear, or their determination behind a mask of engineered composure. This readability is both a weakness and a strength — opponents can read a human fighter's emotional state, but audiences connect with them more immediately and more deeply.

### Notable Characteristics In-Game

Standard silhouette. Baseline proportions against which all other species are visually compared. The most recognizable and immediately relatable character model. Humans serve as the visual anchor that makes other species' divergences legible.

## 2. Humanoids (Divergent)

### Origin

Created through generations of Pantheon-rewarded genetic modification. Human DNA that has been deliberately altered across multiple successive generations until the result is recognizably human-derived but no longer *Homo sapiens*.

The first humanoids were not designed — they emerged. Early recipients of genetic Pantheon rewards passed their modifications to their children, who received further modifications, who passed those enhancements onward. Over three to five generations, the accumulated changes produced individuals who were clearly human in origin but whose physiology had diverged past the threshold of baseline humanity. They were taller, faster, sharper-eyed, leaner. Their metabolism processed nutrients differently. Their reflexes operated on a measurably faster timeline.

The term "Divergent" was coined by early geneticists who recognized that these individuals had diverged from the human evolutionary path. It was a clinical description that became a cultural identity.

### Physiology

Taller and leaner than baseline humans, with longer limbs relative to torso length. This is not a deformity — the proportions are elegant, almost sculptural — but it is immediately noticeable. A humanoid standing next to a baseline human appears elongated, like a figure drawn with a slightly different set of proportional rules.

Enhanced reflexes provide measurably faster response times. Superior visual acuity extends into near-infrared ranges. Lower oxygen requirements allow sustained exertion at altitudes and environments that would challenge baseline humans. Metabolic efficiency means humanoids require less food and water for equivalent activity levels.

Skin tones extend beyond the natural human range. Pale blue, deep gold, soft silver, muted violet — these colors appear in humanoid populations with regularity. The effect is beautiful but subtly "off." A humanoid's skin color is clearly not the result of natural melanin distribution. It is something else — something designed.

### Culture

Humanoids often form their own communities, drawn together by shared experience and mutual understanding. These communities exist within larger human-majority settlements and as independent enclaves. Some humanoid communities are deeply integrated with their human neighbors. Others maintain careful distance, protecting their distinct identity.

The question of identity is central to humanoid culture. Some humanoids identify strongly as human — modified humans, certainly, but human nonetheless. They point to their human ancestry, their human emotions, their fundamentally human worldview. Other humanoids see themselves as something new entirely — a distinct species with its own characteristics, its own culture, its own destiny. This internal division is one of the defining tensions in humanoid society.

There is ongoing political tension about whether humanoids should be classified as human for legal and cultural purposes. Territories governed by human majorities have approached this question differently — some grant full legal equality, others maintain categorical distinctions, and a few have enacted restrictive legislation. The humanoid civil rights movement is one of the most significant political forces in the post-Pantheon world.

### Combat Role

Specialists. The humanoid's modified physiology makes them naturally suited for focused combat roles rather than generalist fighting.

Scouts leverage enhanced visual acuity and fast reflexes to read battlefield conditions and respond to threats before opponents can react. Their ability to process visual information at range gives them an advantage in reconnaissance and ranged combat. Endurance fighters exploit efficient metabolism to outlast opponents in prolonged engagements. Close-quarters specialists use their superior reflexes to control the rhythm of fast exchanges.

This specialization comes at a cost. Humanoids are less adaptable than baseline humans. When a fight moves outside their specialty, they lack the raw versatility that human fighters draw upon. A humanoid scout forced into a grinding close-quarters battle is fighting against their own design.

### Why They Fight

Many humanoids feel a deep need to prove they are worthy of existence. Their species was not born — it was made. Every humanoid carries the knowledge that their physiology is the product of deliberate design choices, not natural evolution. This creates a psychological pressure that baseline humans rarely experience: the need to justify one's own creation.

Some humanoids fight to demonstrate excellence, proving that their modifications make them superior. Others fight to protect the human communities that accept them, earning their place through service. A smaller but significant number fight to prove that modification does not determine destiny — that a designed being can still choose their own path.

### Visual Identity

Elongated proportions. Graceful, almost sculptural silhouettes. Skin tones that are beautiful but clearly outside the natural human range — pale blue, deep gold, soft silver. The overall impression is of a figure that could be human but isn't quite. The beauty is real but it carries a faint quality of artificiality, like a masterful painting that is recognizably not a photograph.

### Notable Characteristics In-Game

Taller silhouette than baseline humans. Same hitbox for balance purposes, but visually taller and leaner. The elongated proportions are the most immediately recognizable visual tell. Skin color options extend beyond the human range, providing additional visual distinction.

## 3. Artificial Humans (Engineered)

### Origin

Bio-engineered from scratch using Pantheon bio-enhancement technology. Not modified humans — designed and grown in laboratories. They are the most direct and deliberate application of Pantheon reward technology to the creation of sentient life.

Where humanoids emerged from cumulative generational change, artificial humans are intentional constructions. Every element of their physiology was selected, optimized, and assembled. They have no genetic parents in any meaningful sense. Each one is a custom design, specified by the laboratory that created them, built to serve a purpose defined before their first breath.

The first artificial humans were created within a century of the Pantheon's arrival, as reward technologies advanced to the point where entire organisms could be designed rather than merely modified. They were immediately controversial. The question of whether a designed being possesses the same rights as a born one has never been fully resolved.

### Physiology

Peak human performance in every measurable dimension. Strength, speed, reflexes, endurance, sensory processing — all operate at or above the maximum natural human capacity. An artificial human at rest outperforms a baseline human at peak exertion.

They mature significantly faster than natural humans. Combat readiness is achievable in five to seven years rather than eighteen. This accelerated development includes physical maturation, cognitive development, and combat training absorption. The trade-off is that their total lifespan, while still substantial, tends to be shorter than a medically-extended baseline human — the accelerated biology burns through its reserves more quickly.

Each artificial human is a unique design. Laboratories do not produce copies — they produce individuals, each with a specified combination of capabilities. Some are optimized for raw physical power. Others are designed for speed and precision. Still others emphasize sensory processing or cognitive function. The design space is vast, and no two artificial humans are exactly alike.

### Culture

Artificial humans are the most politically and ethically controversial species. Their existence raises fundamental questions about the nature of personhood, the ethics of designed life, and the limits of acceptable application of Pantheon technology.

Some guilds build artificial humans specifically for Vimana cycles — designing fighters optimized for arena combat, training them from accelerated childhood, and deploying them as professional combatants. Other guilds consider this practice abhorrent, viewing it as a form of slavery dressed in biological clothing. The divide runs deep and cuts across every other political alignment.

Artificial humans themselves hold diverse perspectives. Some embrace their designed purpose with genuine enthusiasm, finding meaning and satisfaction in the role they were built for. Others struggle profoundly with questions of identity and autonomy — the knowledge that their capabilities, their inclinations, and even their personality traits were specified by a design committee creates a unique existential burden. The question "Are we tools or people?" has no single answer within the artificial human community.

Culturally, artificial humans tend to form bonds based on shared origin rather than shared design. Two artificial humans from different laboratories, designed for completely different purposes, will often find more common ground with each other than with any member of another species. The shared experience of being designed — of knowing that your existence was a project — creates a bond that transcends individual differences.

### Combat Role

Peak performers. An artificial human in combat is the closest thing to a perfect fighter that current technology can produce. Their physical capabilities exceed every other species at the individual level.

This exceptional per-capita performance is balanced by limited numbers. Artificial human production is resource-intensive, requiring specialized facilities, advanced Pantheon-derived technology, and significant investment of time and materials. No guild can field an all-artificial-human roster. They are force multipliers, not forces.

Their combat style tends toward mechanical precision. Every movement is optimized. Every strike is placed with exacting accuracy. They do not waste energy on unnecessary motion. Against less disciplined opponents, this efficiency is devastating. Against fighters who can match their technical level, the artificial human's lack of improvisational flexibility can become a vulnerability — they are designed to execute, not to innovate.

### Why They Fight

The reasons vary dramatically, shaped by both design and experience.

Some artificial humans were designed with loyalty protocols — biological inclinations toward service, obedience, and dedication to a specific guild or patron. These individuals fight because they were built to fight, and the question of whether this constitutes free will is one they may never resolve.

Some fight because combat is the only purpose they know. Raised in training environments, socialized among fighters, they have no frame of reference for a life outside the arena. Fighting is not a choice — it is the defining feature of their existence.

And some fight because they have chosen to. These individuals — and their numbers are growing — represent the most philosophically significant development in artificial human culture. They have examined their design, understood their programming, and made a conscious decision to fight anyway. Or to fight differently. Or to fight for something their designers never intended. In doing so, they make the most powerful argument for artificial human personhood: the argument from demonstrated autonomy.

### Visual Identity

"Too perfect." This is the phrase that recurs most often in descriptions of artificial humans.

Their features are symmetrical to an almost uncanny degree. Their proportions are idealized — not the elegant elongation of humanoids, but a classical perfection that seems derived from mathematical principles rather than natural variation. Their skin is flawless, unmarked by scar or blemish. Their movements are mechanically precise, each gesture executed with a quality of intentionality that baseline humans find subtly unsettling.

The overall impression is of a human designed by committee — every feature optimized, every variable tuned, every imperfection eliminated. The result is beautiful in the same way that a precision-engineered instrument is beautiful. It is impressive. It is admirable. And it is faintly wrong in a way that is difficult to articulate but immediately felt.

In low-light conditions, some artificial humans exhibit a faint luminescence — a subtle glow that is not quite bioluminescence and not quite electronic. It is a side effect of their enhanced cellular metabolism, and it serves as one of the most reliable visual tells for the species.

### Notable Characteristics In-Game

The most "synthetic" visual appearance of any species. Subtle visual tells distinguish them from baseline humans: skin that is too smooth, proportions that are too precise, features that are too symmetrical. The faint luminescence in low-light environments provides an additional distinctive element.

## 4. Cybersymbiotes (Hybrid)

### Origin

Humans who have bonded with a symbiotic machine organism — a technology derived from Pantheon cybernetic rewards. The symbiote is not a tool or an implant. It is a living machine entity that integrates with the host's nervous system on a fundamental level, creating a hybrid organism that is neither purely human nor purely machine.

The bonding process was discovered rather than designed. Early experiments with Pantheon cybernetic rewards occasionally produced unexpected results — machines that didn't just interface with human biology but merged with it. These mergers were initially considered failures. It took decades for researchers to recognize that the resulting hybrid organisms were stable, functional, and in many ways superior to both their human hosts and their machine predecessors.

The symbiote itself is derived from Pantheon technology but has evolved beyond its original parameters. Modern symbiotes are grown, not manufactured. Each one develops unique characteristics during the bonding process, adapting to its specific host in ways that cannot be fully predicted or controlled.

### Physiology

Human base form with visible cybernetic integration. The symbiote manifests as circuitry-like patterns on the skin — not tattoos or surface markings, but visible expressions of the machine network that has merged with the host's nervous and circulatory systems. These patterns often glow faintly, their brightness and color fluctuating with the host's emotional and physical state.

Mechanical augmentations are visible at joints, along the spine, and sometimes at the temples. These are not external prosthetics — they are integrated components, grown into the host's skeleton and musculature. The symbiote reinforces joint structure, enhances muscular response, and provides secondary structural support.

The bonding is permanent. Removing the symbiote from a bonded host is fatal — the two organisms have become interdependent at a systemic level. The symbiote cannot survive without the host's biological processes, and the host cannot survive without the symbiote's cybernetic regulation.

The symbiote and host share consciousness to varying degrees. In most cases, the boundary between human thought and machine processing is blurred but intact — the host remains recognizably themselves, with the symbiote functioning as a secondary cognitive system that provides enhanced processing, multitasking, and sensory integration. In rare cases, the boundary dissolves entirely, producing a merged consciousness that is neither human nor machine but something genuinely new.

### Culture

Cybersymbiotes are the rarest of the four species, and rarity breeds suspicion. They are feared more than any other modified human variant — not because of what they do, but because of what they represent. A cybersymbiote is a human who has surrendered part of their autonomy to a machine. The host is still present, still conscious, still making decisions — but those decisions are now influenced, shaped, and sometimes overridden by a non-human intelligence.

The bonding process itself carries significant risk. Not all candidates survive. Those who do are fundamentally changed — they process information differently, move differently, perceive the world differently. Former friends and family members often describe the feeling of talking to someone they recognize but no longer fully know. The host's personality persists, but it has been... translated. Shifted. Extended in ways that are difficult to articulate.

Some communities see cybersymbiotes as the next stage of human evolution — the logical endpoint of a trajectory that began with the first Pantheon rewards. Others see them as abominations, grotesque fusions of flesh and machine that have abandoned their humanity. Most people fall somewhere between these extremes, responding to individual cybersymbiotes with a mixture of fascination and discomfort.

Cybersymbiote communities exist but are small and widely scattered. The shared experience of the bond creates an immediate understanding between cybersymbiotes that transcends cultural and geographic differences. They recognize each other as kin — not by blood, but by the common experience of sharing one's mind with a machine.

### Combat Role

Enhanced cognitive processing and multitasking define the cybersymbiote's combat advantage. Where artificial humans excel through physical superiority, cybersymbiotes dominate through mental capability.

A cybersymbiote can track multiple targets simultaneously, processing each one as a separate attention stream. They can analyze an opponent's movement patterns in real-time, predicting attacks before they are launched. They can interface directly with weapon systems, achieving integration speeds that are impossible for unaugmented fighters. And they can do all of this while maintaining total situational awareness — the symbiote handles parallel processing while the human consciousness focuses on strategic decision-making.

Their physical augmentation is significant but not overwhelming. The symbiote enhances reaction time, muscular response, and structural integrity, but these improvements are secondary to the cognitive advantages. A cybersymbiote wins fights by thinking faster and processing more information than their opponent — not by being stronger or faster.

### Why They Fight

The symbiote bond often creates a compulsion toward challenge and conflict. The machine organism is, at its core, a combat enhancement — its evolutionary trajectory was shaped by the demands of combat performance, and it expresses its fulfillment through the exercise of combat capabilities. A bonded host who goes too long without physical challenge may experience increasing agitation, restlessness, and eventual psychological distress.

But the human host's motivations persist alongside the symbiote's drives. Many cybersymbiotes fight for the same reasons humans do — duty, honor, personal ambition, community obligation. The symbiote's influence adds urgency to these motivations but does not replace them.

Some cybersymbiotes fight because the alternative is worse. The symbiote does not tolerate idleness well. A host who refuses to engage in combat may find the symbiote's influence becoming more aggressive — pushing harder, demanding more, eroding the host's sense of autonomy. For these individuals, regular combat is a form of maintenance — a way of satisfying the symbiote's demands while preserving their own psychological integrity.

### Visual Identity

The most visually distinct species. Glowing circuitry patterns trace across exposed skin, their light pulsing with the host's heartbeat. Mechanical augmentations are visible at joints and along the spine — integrated components that reinforce and enhance the human skeleton. Eyes sometimes glow or shift color, reflecting the symbiote's processing state.

The overall impression is a blend of organic and mechanical that is simultaneously beautiful and unsettling. The circuitry patterns are aesthetically striking — they follow the body's natural lines, accentuating musculature and bone structure in ways that almost seem intentional. But the glow, the visible mechanical elements, and the occasional flash of inhuman processing behind the eyes remind observers that they are looking at something that is not entirely human.

### Notable Characteristics In-Game

The most recognizable silhouette due to cybernetic elements. Visual tells include glowing accents on the skin, mechanical details at joints and along the spine, and eyes that occasionally shift color or brightness. The visual design communicates the hybrid nature of the species — human form overlaid with machine integration.

## Inter-Species Dynamics

### Social Relations

The four species coexist in a state of perpetual tension. They share cities, economies, and political systems, but they do not share a common understanding of what it means to be human. Every social interaction between members of different species carries the weight of this unresolved question.

Baseline humans occupy a position of demographic majority and cultural default. The world's institutions, traditions, and social norms were designed by humans for humans. Every other species must navigate these structures as adaptations of a system that was not built for them. This creates friction even in the absence of explicit prejudice.

Humanoids experience the most day-to-day social tension. They are close enough to human to be held to human standards, but different enough to regularly fall short of those standards in ways that feel unfair. A humanoid's elongated proportions draw stares. Their unconventional skin tones prompt questions. Their enhanced capabilities provoke resentment. They are too human to be exotic, too different to be accepted.

Artificial humans face a different kind of social challenge. Their existence is often treated as a philosophical problem rather than a personal reality. People discuss whether artificial humans have souls, whether they deserve rights, whether their creation was ethical — often within earshot of artificial humans who are expected to endure this treatment with the mechanical patience they were stereotypically designed to possess.

Cybersymbiotes experience the most visceral social reactions. People flinch at the glow of their circuitry. They avoid eye contact. They keep their distance. The fear is not rational — cybersymbiotes are no more dangerous than any other species — but it is deeply felt. The sight of a human being who has merged with a machine touches something primal in the human psyche.

### Political Landscape

The political landscape is defined by the tension between two broad movements: **Purism** and **Integrationism**.

**Purism** holds that baseline humanity is the standard against which all other species should be measured. Purist positions range from moderate (modified species deserve legal protection but not legal equivalence) to extreme (all non-baseline species should be restricted, segregated, or eliminated). Purist sentiment is strongest in human-majority territories and tends to intensify during periods of economic stress or social instability.

**Integrationism** holds that all human-origin species are equally valid expressions of humanity. Integrationist positions range from moderate (all species deserve equal legal status) to radical (the concept of "species" should be abolished entirely in favor of a unified post-human identity). Integrationist sentiment is strongest in territories with high species diversity and in academic and cultural institutions.

Neither movement is monolithic, and neither maps cleanly onto any single species. There are human integrationists and humanoid purists. There are artificial humans who argue for strict species boundaries and cybersymbiotes who advocate for universal bonding. The political landscape is defined by intersection and contradiction, not by clean alignments.

### Guild Integration

Fighter guilds are among the most integrated institutions in the post-Pantheon world. Combat effectiveness transcends species boundaries, and guilds that maintain species-based exclusion policies tend to perform poorly in Vimana cycles compared to integrated competitors.

Most major guilds are mixed-species organizations. Their training programs are designed to account for each species' physiological and cognitive characteristics. Human fighters train alongside humanoids, artificial humans, and cybersymbiotes. Guilds develop internal cultures that — at their best — emphasize capability and commitment over species origin.

This does not mean guilds are free of inter-species tension. Competition for roster spots, training resources, and patron attention often breaks along species lines. Human fighters sometimes resent the natural advantages of modified species. Humanoids sometimes feel pressured to specialize in roles that match their physiological profile. Artificial humans sometimes struggle with the assumption that they are inherently more valuable due to their peak performance. Cybersymbiotes sometimes face fear and distrust from their own teammates.

The guilds that manage these tensions most effectively are the ones that have developed strong internal cultures — shared values, traditions, and identity markers that override species divisions. In these guilds, membership is more important than origin.

### The Pantheon's Perspective

The Pantheon's attitude toward species divergence is characteristically opaque. They do not comment on the ethics of modification, engineering, or bonding. They do not express preferences for one species over another. They reward performance in the Vimana cycle without regard for which species the performer belongs to.

Some observers interpret this neutrality as indifference — the Pantheon does not care about species distinctions because the Pantheon does not care about individual human welfare. Others interpret it as approval — the Pantheon rewards the technologies that create species divergence, so they must view divergence as desirable. Still others see it as a test — the Pantheon has given humanity the tools to reshape itself and is observing what humanity chooses to become.

The only direct evidence of Pantheon awareness of species distinctions is the Vimana cycle itself. The cycle's rules apply equally to all species, and the arenas are designed to accommodate the full range of physiological variation. This suggests that the Pantheon acknowledges species differences as a practical reality while refusing to treat them as a basis for distinction.

Whether this is wisdom, indifference, or something else entirely remains a matter of debate.

## Character Archetypes

The following archetypes represent recurring character patterns within the world of JuanSchott. They are not finalized named characters — specific character development will occur in seasonal content — but they provide a foundation for understanding the types of stories that emerge from this world.

### The Veteran

**Species:** Human (Baseline)

A human fighter who has survived multiple Vimana cycles. They have fought against and alongside every other species. They have won and lost and won again. Their body carries the accumulated damage of a career in arena combat — scars, chronic injuries, the subtle wrongness of joints that have been repaired too many times.

The Veteran represents the persistence of human capability in a world of engineered perfection. They win not because they are the strongest or the fastest, but because they have seen everything and adapted to all of it. Their experience is their augmentation. Their survival is their argument.

They are respected by every species. Even artificial humans — designed for combat excellence — recognize that there is something in the Veteran's longevity that raw capability cannot replicate. The Veteran has something that cannot be engineered: the accumulated wisdom of a thousand fights, survived.

### The Prototype

**Species:** Artificial Human (Engineered)

The first of a new generation — an artificial human whose design incorporates innovations not present in any previous model. They are faster, stronger, and more capable than any engineered fighter before them. They are also the most closely watched.

The Prototype carries the weight of expectation. Their creators have invested enormous resources in their design. Their guild has built promotional campaigns around their debut. Other artificial humans view them with a mixture of hope and resentment — hope that a successful prototype will advance the standing of all engineered people, and resentment that they are expected to celebrate a design rather than a person.

The Prototype's central conflict is the tension between design and identity. They were created to be the best. But what happens when being the best is no longer enough? What happens when the Prototype wants something that wasn't in the design specification?

### The Bonded

**Species:** Cybersymbiote (Hybrid)

A cybersymbiote whose symbiote has developed unexpected autonomy. The machine organism has begun expressing preferences, making suggestions, and occasionally acting independently of the host's conscious direction. This is not supposed to happen — symbiotes are not designed for independent thought.

The Bonded represents the most radical implication of the cybersymbiote concept. If the symbiote can think for itself, then the host is not augmented — they are shared. The Bonded must negotiate with their own augmentation, treating the symbiote as a partner rather than a tool. This creates challenges that no other species faces: the challenge of coexistence with a non-human intelligence that shares your body and your mind.

The Bonded's combat style reflects this partnership. They fight with a fluidity that other cybersymbiotes lack — the symbiote contributes tactical insight while the host provides strategic direction, and the result is a fighting style that is genuinely collaborative. Opponents find the Bonded difficult to predict because there are two minds making decisions, not one.

### The Exile

**Species:** Humanoid (Divergent)

A humanoid who has left their community to fight independently — without guild affiliation, without patron support, without the social safety net that humanoid communities provide. They have chosen isolation over integration, independence over belonging.

The Exile represents the humanoid identity crisis in its most acute form. They have rejected both the human world that will not fully accept them and the humanoid community that offers belonging at the cost of conformity. They fight alone because they have not found a place where they can be both humanoid and individual.

Their combat style reflects this independence. The Exile has developed techniques that no guild would teach — unconventional, adaptive, and deeply personal. They are a specialist who has learned to generalize through necessity, combining the humanoid's natural specialization with a self-taught versatility that makes them unpredictable.

### The Witness

**Species:** Human (Baseline) — Not a fighter

A historian and documentarian who records the Vimana cycles from the inside. They travel with guilds, observe training, interview fighters, and compile accounts of the cycles for posterity. They do not fight. They watch.

The Witness represents the perspective that the fighting species cannot provide. They see the cycles without the filter of combat experience. They notice the details that fighters miss — the political maneuvering between guilds, the economic pressures that shape rosters, the cultural currents that determine which fighters become champions and which are forgotten.

The Witness is important because they preserve the record. In a world defined by combat spectacle, someone must document what the spectacle means. The Witness ensures that the cycles are remembered as events that happened to people, not merely as competitions that produced winners and losers.
